    Discover Renault

    Motorsport History

    History and culture are closely linked to Renault motorsport, which is part of the DNA of the brand. For over 115 years, Renault has always been a major player in motorsport.

    Early beginnings

    Company founder Louis Renault was well aware of how sporting exploits could shape brand image. On 24 December 1898, he decided the best way to prove the superiority of his first major innovation —direct-drive transmission— would be to climb the steep Rue Lepic in the Montmartre district of Paris, at the wheel of his Voiturette Type A. The challenge paid off, and motor sport would become an important means of expression for Renault ever since. In the early years of the twentieth century, Automobiles Renault would boast a run of Grand Prix victories —such as Paris-Trouville in 1899 and Paris-Vienne in 1902— with a Renault Type K powered by the first Renault-designed engine, plus the Dieppe Automobile Club prize in 1913. Motor sport in Europe ceased with the First World War, but when peace came, Renault was soon back. In the nineteen-twenties, motor sport was dominated by the impressive Renault 40CV, which claimed its first victory in the 1925 Monte-Carlo Rally and went on to break many a racetrack speed record.

    After the Second World War, Renault refocused production on more compact cars with smaller engines, but even these models would achieve illustrious sporting honours. The Renault 4CV proved immensely successful, as did its successor, the Dauphine. Then the Renault 8 Gordini opened a new dimension that would take Renault to the pinnacles of motor sport.

    First Gordini...And then Alpine

    In 1958, Renault chose the Italian-born Amédée Gordini to head its motor sport division. One of Gordini’s creations, the mythical Renault 8 Gordini, would earn the unparalleled appreciation and affection of a whole generation of drivers, excelling in rallies, hill-climb and racetrack meetings alike. The car proved so immensely popular as to warrant the emergence, in 1966, of the Renault 8 Gordini Cup, a programme widely considered to be the forerunner of brand-specific championships. Along similar lines, launch of the Renault 12 Gordini was celebrated by the G-Day event on 18 and 19 July 1970: a huge gathering of Gordini owners at the Paul Ricard racetrack. The spirit of this extraordinary meeting lives on to this day, through the World Series by Renault.

    Around the same period, the Formula Renault event came into being, run by the first cars to be powered by the Renault 12 Gordini engine. The first Formula Renault French championship was held in 1971.
     

    And then Alpine ...

    In 1973, Renault took a majority stake in Alpine, a small company that Renault had partnered for some time. This would be a great year indeed, with the Alpine A110 Berlinette winning the very first World Rally Championship.

    The early days of Formula 1

    The origins of Renault Sport date to when Renault hired Amédée Gordini (who had previously made Grand Prix racers under his own name) to design racing cars, which would be built at a brand new plant in Viry-Châtillon, on the outskirts of Paris.

    To start with, Renault would concentrate on a two-litre V6 engine, unveiled in January 1973. This unit proved its worth in the prestigious European championship of two-litre sportscars, then Renault joined the FIA Sportscar Championship and set about developing a turbocharged version of its engine.

    Renault Sport was officially founded in 1976, the same year that saw the emergence of a single-seater racing programme, in the form of the European Formula Two Championship, followed by the Le Mans Sportscar Championship, then the Formula One Championship in 1977. Renault started out in Formula One as a racing team, pioneering a major innovation in the form of a turbocharged V6 engine, to run against the normally aspirated power plants of its rivals.

    Meanwhile, Didier Pironi and Jean-Pierre Jaussaud won the 1978 Le Mans 24-Hour event in an Alpine-Renault A442B powered by Renault’s turbocharged V6 engine.

    Renault claimed its first Formula One victory in 1979, at the French Grand Prix, with Jean-Pierre Jabouille driving the RS11. In the early eighties, Renault would win no fewer than 15 Grands Prix, and finished second in the 1983 World Championship with Alain Prost, before withdrawing from Formula One at the end of the 1985 season. With its innovative turbocharger technology, Renault had defied the prevailing scepticism to win through yet again.

    The start of an exceptional Formula One record

    Renault returned to Formula One in the late eighties, but this time as an engine builder, with the brilliant V10 standing out above the competition and enabling the Williams and Benetton teams to dominate the discipline. Renault engines were unbeaten from 1992 to 1997, winning 85 Grands Prix, six manufacturers’ championship titles and five drivers’ titles (with Nigel Mansell, Alain Prost, Michael Schumacher, Damon Hill and Jacques Villeneuve).

    Rallying to promote the brand

    Renault was hardly less active on the rally front. Renault won the manufacturer’s title in the 1974 World Rally Championship, then Guy Fréquelin won the 1977 French Rally Championship at the wheel of the Alpine A310 Group 5. Then the Renault 5 Alpine acquired stunning renown with Jean Ragnotti, finishing second in the 1978 Monte-Carlo Rally. The Renault 5 Turbo proved highly successful, winning the 1981 Monte-Carlo Rally and the 1985 Tour de Corse, again with Jean Ragnotti.

    Renault continued its rally involvement throughout the nineties, considering it an ideal platform for promoting its cars. After various Clio versions (including the 16S, Williams and Maxi), enter the Maxi Mégane, with which Philippe Bugalski added his name to the list of Tour de Corse winners in 1997.

    The Clio Super 1600 also enjoyed considerable success in rally racing, winning several international titles from 2003 to 2005.

    Return to Formula 1

    Renault returned to Formula One in 2001, buying the Benetton team, to be renamed Renault F1 Team in 2002. The rise of the new team coincided with the arrival of a certain Fernando Alonso, world champion driver two years running, in 2005 and 2006, with Renault F1 Team world champion manufacturer. In 2011, the team was sold to Genii Capital; Renault wished to concentrate on its engine-building operations, powering other teams’ racers. Renault powered the Red Bull Racing team to world championship victory, winning three manufacturers’ championship titles from 2010 to 2013 and enabling Sebastian Vettel to enter motor sports legend by becoming the youngest four-time world champion in the history of Formula One.

    World Series by Renault

    World Series by Renault is celebrating its tenth season in 2014. The programme was started in 2005, from the merger of Eurocup Formula Renault V6 and World Series by Nissan.

    World Series by Renault meetings combine top-level competitions, Formula One or Renault 5 Maxi Turbo shows, and a wealth of family entertainment features.

    The Formula Renault 3.5 and Formula Renault 2.0 championship races run at World Series by Renault meetings form a fantastic springboard for future Formula One drivers. Some 60% of today’s Formula One drivers —including Sebastian Vettel, Romain Grosjean and Daniel Ricciardo— have a track background that includes Formula Renault events.

    Other races run at World Series by Renault meetings include Eurocup Clio and Formula Renault 2.0 and Formula Renault 1.6 national championships.

    European Endurance with Alpine

    In 2013, Alpine signed a victorious return to motor sport: the A450 run by the Signatech team finished first in the LMP2 category in the European endurance championship.

    Then thirty-five years (day for day) after topping the overall ranking in 1978, Alpine was back in the 24-hour Le Mans event, thrilling the audience by overcoming initial difficulties to reach an altogether impressive 14th place.

    A new challenge: Formula E

    Given its proven primacy in motor sport, plus its unique expertise in electric vehicles, it was only natural that Renault should run in FIA’s new Formula E championship. Renault has been a technical partner of Formula E Holding since May 2013, and contributes to development work on the 100% electric Spark-Renault SRT_01E racer.
